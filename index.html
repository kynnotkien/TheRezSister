The Res Sisters

By Tomson Highway

Directed by: Alanis King

Starring: Lisa C. Ravensbergen, Tracey Nepinak, Tantoo Cardinal, Lisa Cromarty, Tracey Cook, and PJ Prudat

Produced by: Native Earth Performing Arts

Location: Aki Studio, Daniels Spectrum, 585 Dundas St. E., Toronto

Runs: Until February 19, 2017

Rating: 4/5

This play was first performed in Toronto, Canada on November 26, 1986 at the Native Earth Performing Arts Inc.,. The play toured Canada from October to February of 1988 with sold-out audiences. This success helped the play become one of only two Canadian plays to be showcased at the Edinburgh International Festival in August of 1988. The play also won playwright Tomson Highway the Dora Mavor Moore Award for best new play in 1986.

Tomson Highway

Tomson Highway is a renowned Canadian playwright, novelist, and musician of Cree ancestry. He is celebrated for his insightful and humorous exploration of Indigenous themes, particularly the experiences of First Nations people in Canada.

Born: December 6, 1951, in northwestern Manitoba, Canada

Summary

The play The Rez Sisters revolves around the lives of seven indigenous women on the Wasaychigan Hill Indian reserve in Manitoba, Canada. The story focuses on their journey together as they set off to Toronto to attend the world's biggest bingo game in hopes of winning a life-changing grand prize.

The journey to Toronto becomes a metaphor for Indigenous women's search for identity, desire for freedom, and struggle to overcome adversity. Below are the main contents of the play:

  • Dreams and hopes: Each character has his own dreams and hopes. Pelajia dreams of a life of luxury in Toronto, Philomena wants a decent home, Marie-Adele yearns for love and security, Annie wishes to be famous, Emily seeks liberation from her painful past , Véronique longs for wealth, and Zhaboonigan simply wants to be loved and have fun.
  • Harsh reality: Life on the reservation is full of hardship and injustice. Women face poverty, unemployment, racism, domestic violence, and lack of opportunity. Their dreams are often crushed by harsh realities.
  • Culture and identity: The play clearly portrays the culture and identity of indigenous people through language, music, costumes, and spiritual elements. The image of Nanabush, a mythical figure in indigenous culture, appears throughout the play as a reminder of roots and traditions.
  • Humor and tragedy: The play intertwines humorous and ironic details with tragic and emotional moments. Laughter and tears blend together, creating a true picture of the lives of indigenous women.

    • The end of the play leaves many thoughts for the audience. Will the women achieve their dreams? Will they find happiness? And more importantly, can they retain their identity and culture in modern society? The play The Rez Sisters is a powerful work, giving readers insight into the lives and souls of Indigenous women.

    Pelajia Patchnose

    One of the main characters, lives on the roof and dreams of going to Toronto.

    Expressing dissatisfaction with current life and longing for a better life.

    Somewhat childish, likes to tease and quarrel with his younger sister Philomena.

    Philomena Moosetail

    Pelajia's younger sister, more realistic and somewhat pessimistic.

    Often at odds with Pelajia over her sister's dreams and plans.

    Worry about her sister and want her to be happy.

    Marie-Adele Starblanket

    Pelajia and Philomena's half-sister.

    Somewhat naive and dreamy, often talks to seagulls.

    Has a mentally disabled adopted daughter named Zhaboonigan.

    Annie Cook

    Half-sister of the three sisters above.

    Dynamic, cheerful and optimistic, always eager for new things.

    Somewhat curious and gossipy.

    Emily Dictionary

    Annie's sister.

    Quiet and somewhat mysterious, often keeping distance from people.

    Has experienced past trauma and has strong self-defense abilities.

    Veronique St. Pierre

    Sister-in-law of the four sisters above.

    Cruel, judgmental and opinionated, often in conflict with others.

    Dissatisfied with the boring life on the reserve.

    Zhaboonigan Peterson

    Marie-Adele's adopted daughter, mentally disabled.

    Innocent, naive and lovely, bringing lightness to the play.

    Regularly converses with birds and has a special connection with nature.

    Nanabush

    A mythical figure in indigenous culture, who acts as a trickster.

    Appears in the play as a dancer, connecting the real world and the spiritual world.

    Review of the play The Rez Sisters

    Tomson Highway’s The Rez Sisters is a colourful and emotional play that chronicles the lives of seven Indigenous women on the Wasaychigan Hill reserve. Revolving around a dream of a new life after winning a big bingo jackpot in Toronto, the play explores the thorny and tragicomic journey of Indigenous women in contemporary Canadian society.

    The play’s strength lies in its realistic and vivid portrayal of indigenous culture and identity. Language, costumes, customs, myths, etc. are cleverly integrated into the dialogue and actions of the characters, creating a diverse and unique picture of the spiritual life of the indigenous community.

    However, the play still has some limitations. The psychological development of the characters is quite simple and lacks depth. While each woman has her own dreams and hopes, their motivations and inner turmoil are not fully explored. The open ending with the iconic broken-down car is abrupt and unsatisfying.The role of Nanabush, the mythological figure, is also underexploited.

    Overall, The Rez Sisters is a meaningful play about the lives of Indigenous people in Canada. The work raises questions about dreams and hopes, deadlocks and disappointments, sisterhood and family, cultural identity, and the place of women in society. Despite some limitations, the play is still worth watching and pondering.
